Review by Christopher Small

This ebullient rural comedy about horse racing is one of the key Boris Barnet films—mostly for all the ways it shows how out of sync he was with the prevailing trends in Soviet filmmaking circa 1940. It unfolds like one of the giant paper scrolls Barnet used to work from on set to keep track of the story they were inventing as they went along: one improvised scene flows into and justifies the next, often gleefully at the expense of narrative structure.

Like Leo McCarey, Barnet was a comedy director who saw every movie as a cascade of lifelike bits of business; if each piece of the messy puzzle contradicted the last one, tonally as well as ideologically, well, so… more
