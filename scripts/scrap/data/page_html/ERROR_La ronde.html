La Ronde

1950

Directed by Max Ophüls

Synopsis

An all-knowing interlocutor guides us through a series of affairs in Vienna, 1900. A soldier meets an eager young lady of the evening. Later he has an affair with a young lady, who becomes a maid and does similarly with the young man of the house. The young man seduces a married woman. On and on, spinning on the gay carousel of life.

“La Ronde” marks the sparkling return of director Max Ophüls to Europe following the end of the Second World War. It is a fantasy of light and love that captures a wonder that had lain dormant under the weight of conflict.

Several of Max Ophuls’ films might be seen as the cinematic equivalents of souffles. None is more airily delicate than La Ronde. It’s almost as if it would collapse if you frowned at it. But it’s a great film, a wonderful example of style over substance.

And such style. Max’s camera is constantly moving, with several lovely tracking shots. He may also prove a greater variety of camera angles than in any other romantic film.
